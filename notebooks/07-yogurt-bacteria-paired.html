Group A Yogurt Study — Paired Bacteria Analysis

How bacteria levels changed from baseline to after antibiotics, by arm

Study Notes (read this first!)

Aim of the study

The main goal of this study was to find out whether eating yoghurt helps protect the vaginal microbes (bacteria) when a person takes antibiotics. Antibiotics can kill both the bad bacteria causing an infection AND the good bacteria that keep the vagina healthy, which can lead to problems like yeast infections or bacterial vaginosis. The idea is that yoghurt (which contains good bacteria like Lactobacillus) might help restore or protect the vaginal microbiome.

Objectives

  1. Measure the levels of key vaginal bacteria at baseline (before any antibiotics) and after taking antibiotics
  2. Compare what happens in two groups of participants:
    • Unchanged diet — people who went about their normal diet
    • Yoghurt — people who ate yoghurt during the study
  3. See if the yoghurt group had better preservation of “good” bacteria (L. crispatus) compared to the unchanged diet group
  4. Use statistical tests (p-values) to decide if any differences we see are real or just by chance

How the study was conducted

Here’s a simple walkthrough of the study design:

Step 1: Recruit participants (total N = 51 women)
                |
Step 2: Divide into two arms
        ├── Unchanged diet (~25 women) — eat as usual
        └── Yoghurt (~26 women) — eat yoghurt daily
                |
Step 3: Take baseline sample (swab) → measure bacteria
                |
Step 4: Everyone takes antibiotics (for an infection)
                |
Step 5: Take after-antibiotic sample → measure bacteria again
                |
Step 6: Compare: Did bacteria change? Did yoghurt help?

Key idea — “Paired” design: Each woman is measured twice (before and after antibiotics). This is powerful because each person acts as her own control. We’re not comparing different people — we’re comparing each person to herself before and after. This removes a lot of natural differences between people (everyone’s microbiome is a bit different).

What we measured (the bacteria)

The researchers used a technique called qPCR (quantitative PCR) to count how many copies of bacterial DNA were in each sample. Think of it like a microscope that can count individual bacteria. They looked at:

Bacteria column What it means Why it matters
qpcr_bacteria Total bacteria — all bacteria combined Gives us a sense of the overall bacterial community
qpcr_crispatus Lactobacillus crispatus — a “good” bacteria This species produces hydrogen peroxide which kills bad bacteria. High levels = healthy vaginal environment
qpcr_iners Lactobacillus iners — another Lactobacillus species Less protective than crispatus. Can switch between helpful and harmful depending on conditions

How we made the graphs (explained simply)

Step 1 — Gather the data: We read three CSV files — one with the participant IDs and which group they were in, one with the time point (baseline vs after antibiotic), and one with the qPCR bacteria counts. We merged them together so every row has: who the person is + when the sample was taken + which group they belong to + how much bacteria was found.

Step 2 — Organise the data: We reshaped the data so each bacteria (Total, L. crispatus, L. iners) becomes its own row. This makes it easier to create one graph per bacteria.

Step 3 — Create the plots: For each bacteria, we drew: - Grey lines connecting each person’s two measurements (baseline → after antibiotic). Each line is one woman. If the line goes down, her bacteria decreased. If it goes up, it increased. - Box plots showing the overall pattern for the group. The box shows the middle 50% of values, the line inside is the median (middle value), and the “whiskers” show the range. - Dots for each individual measurement, coloured by arm. - P-values calculated using a paired Wilcoxon test. This is a statistical test that asks: “Is the change from before to after antibiotics big enough that we can be confident it’s not just random noise?”

Step 4 — Build the tables: For each bacteria and each arm, we counted how many participants had detectable levels (n, %), calculated the median (middle value) and IQR (spread), and added the p-value.

Understanding p-values (simple explanation)

A p-value tells you the probability that what you’re seeing happened by random chance alone.

  • p < 0.05 — less than 5% chance it’s random. We call this “statistically significant”. The difference is probably real.
  • p < 0.01 — less than 1% chance. Strong evidence of a real difference.
  • p < 0.001 — less than 0.1% chance. Very strong evidence.
  • p > 0.05 — not statistically significant. The difference could easily be due to random chance. We can’t confidently say there’s a real difference.

Note: A high p-value doesn’t mean “no difference”. It means “we don’t have enough evidence to say there IS a difference”. With only ~25 people per group, sometimes real differences are missed because the sample is too small.

Advantages of this study design

Advantage Why it’s good
Paired design Each person is their own control. This removes the natural variation between different people, making it easier to spot real changes
Two time points We see the change over time, not just a snapshot. This tells us about the effect of antibiotics + yoghurt
qPCR measurement qPCR is very precise — it can detect tiny amounts of bacterial DNA, giving accurate counts
Wilcoxon test This test doesn’t assume the data follows a bell curve (normal distribution). Our data has many zeros and is very skewed, so this is the right choice
Detection rates We show both how many people have the bacteria AND how much. This gives a complete picture

Disadvantages and limitations

Limitation Why it matters
Small sample size About 25 people per arm. With small groups, it’s harder to detect real differences (low “statistical power”)
No randomisation info We don’t know how people were assigned to yoghurt vs unchanged diet. If it wasn’t random, the groups might be different in ways that affect the results
Short follow-up Only one time point after antibiotics. We don’t know if changes lasted or if people recovered
No placebo People knew whether they were eating yoghurt or not. This could affect behaviour (placebo effect)
Many zeros L. crispatus especially has many zero values (not detected). This makes statistical analysis harder and means median values are often 0
Single measurement method Only qPCR was used. Other methods (like 16S sequencing) might give a broader picture of all bacteria present
Confounding factors Things like age, birth control, sexual activity, and diet outside yoghurt could influence the results. We haven’t adjusted for these in these simple plots

Summary and key takeaways

Based on the graphs and tables in this document:

  1. Total bacteria — After antibiotics, total bacteria levels tend to decrease in both groups. The p-values will tell us if the decrease is significant and whether the yoghurt group fared better.

  2. L. crispatus — This is the key “good” bacteria we care about. Antibiotics often wipe it out. If yoghurt helps, we might see crispatus levels dropping less (or recovering more) in the yoghurt group. However, many participants start with zero, which makes it hard to show protection.

  3. L. iners — This species is more mixed in its effects. It might increase after antibiotics as it fills the space left by other bacteria. The yoghurt group might show different patterns.

  4. Overall — The paired plots (grey lines connecting before → after) are the most informative: they show individual trajectories. Look for patterns where most lines go in one direction (all down or all up) — that’s a strong signal. If lines go every which way, there’s no clear effect.

What to look for in each graph: - Do most grey lines go down? → Bacteria decreased after antibiotics - Is the decrease smaller in the yoghurt group? → Yoghurt might help - Is the p-value < 0.05? → The change is statistically significant - Are the detection rates similar at both time points? → Bacteria were preserved

Why this matters

Vaginal health is important for preventing infections, especially in women. If something as simple as eating yoghurt can protect the vaginal microbiome during antibiotic treatment, it could be an easy, low-cost recommendation for doctors to give their patients. This study is a step toward understanding that question.

# Helper function: compute paired Wilcoxon p-values per arm
compute_paired_pvals <- function(data) {
  paired_data <- data %>%
    select(pid, arm, bacteria, time_point, value) %>%
    pivot_wider(names_from = time_point, values_from = value)

  paired_data %>%
    group_by(arm) %>%
    summarise(
      p = wilcox.test(Baseline, `After antibiotic`, paired = TRUE, exact = FALSE)$p.value,
      .groups = "drop"
    ) %>%
    mutate(
      label = paste0("Paired Wilcoxon p = ", formatC(p, format = "e", digits = 2)),
      x_pos = 2.3
    )
}

      ")"

    after_median_iqr = paste0(

    ),

  )

Master summary: All bacteria combined

# Build one big table for all bacteria
build_bacteria_table <- function(bacteria_name) {
  wide <- df_long %>%
    filter(bacteria == bacteria_name) %>%
    select(pid, arm, time_point, value, detected) %>%
    pivot_wider(
      names_from  = time_point,
      values_from = c(value, detected),
      names_glue  = "{time_point}_{.value}"
    )

  detect <- wide %>%
    group_by(arm) %>%
    summarise(
      n_total            = n(),
      baseline_detected  = sum(Baseline_detected == "Detected"),
      baseline_pct       = round(baseline_detected / n_total * 100, 0),
      after_detected     = sum(`After antibiotic_detected` == "Detected"),
      after_pct          = round(after_detected / n_total * 100, 0),
      .groups = "drop"
    ) %>%
    mutate(
      baseline_lbl = paste0(baseline_detected, "/", n_total, " (", baseline_pct, "%)"),
      after_lbl    = paste0(after_detected, "/", n_total, " (", after_pct, "%)")
    )

  summary <- wide %>%
    group_by(arm) %>%
    summarise(
      bl_med_iqr = paste0(
        format(median(Baseline_value, na.rm = TRUE), big.mark = ",", scientific = FALSE),
        " (",
        format(IQR(Baseline_value, na.rm = TRUE), big.mark = ",", scientific = FALSE),
        ")"
      ),
      aft_med_iqr = paste0(
        format(median(`After antibiotic_value`, na.rm = TRUE), big.mark = ",", scientific = FALSE),
        " (",
        format(IQR(`After antibiotic_value`, na.rm = TRUE), big.mark = ",", scientific = FALSE),
        ")"
      ),
      .groups = "drop"
    )

  pval <- wide %>%
    group_by(arm) %>%
    summarise(
      p = wilcox.test(Baseline_value, `After antibiotic_value`, paired = TRUE, exact = FALSE)$p.value,
      .groups = "drop"
    ) %>%
    mutate(p_lbl = formatC(p, format = "e", digits = 2))

  detect %>%
    left_join(summary, by = "arm") %>%
    left_join(pval, by = "arm") %>%
    mutate(bacteria = bacteria_name, .before = 1)
}

master <- bind_rows(
