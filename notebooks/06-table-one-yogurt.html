What the p-values mean here: These tests check whether the baseline characteristics differ between the two arms. In a randomised study, we expect most to be non-significant (p > 0.05), because randomisation should balance the groups. A significant p-value would be a red flag — it might mean randomisation didn’t work perfectly, or the difference is just bad luck (that’s why we usually don’t “adjust” for these, we just report them).

Which test does add_p() pick? It depends on the variable type: - Continuous (age, days_since_last_sex): Wilcoxon rank-sum test (Mann-Whitney U) — a non-parametric test that doesn’t assume normality. - Categorical (education, birth_control, arm): Fisher’s exact test (when any expected count < 5) or chi-squared test.

Step 3 — Override age to mean (SD) + t-test

Sometimes people prefer mean ± SD for age (it’s conventional in many fields). You can override both the summary statistic and the test:

# step 3: override age to mean (SD) + t-test
metadata |>
  select(-pid, -sex) |>
  tbl_summary(
    by = arm,
    statistic = list(age ~ "{mean} ({sd})")
  ) |>
  add_p(
    test = list(age ~ "t.test")
  )

Step 5 — Your interpretation

Now look at the p-values in your final table and answer:

  1. Age — Are the yogurt and unchanged-diet groups similar in age? What’s the p-value?
  2. Education — Does one group have more participants with post-secondary education?
  3. Birth control — Is Depoprovera use balanced between arms?
  4. Days since last sex — Any imbalance here? (This one is sometimes not perfectly balanced even in randomised trials.)

If all p-values are > 0.05, the randomisation did its job — the groups look comparable at baseline. Any differences you see after the intervention can more confidently be attributed to the yogurt, not to pre-existing differences.
